  • LIPIDS (fats or oils) Classification


1. Triacylglycerols
    -- Majority of fatty acids in biological systems are found in the form of triacylglycerols

    -- The primary storage form of lipids (body fat) 

    -- Solubility and density


          -- Fats as efficient fuel storage (compared to carbohydrates)
                -- Energy storage: Fats (long term) vs. polysaccharides (short term, e.g. glycogen)
                -- Fatty acids carry more energy per carbon because they are more reduced
                -- Glucose and glycogen are for short-term energy needs, quick delivery
                -- Fats are for long-term (months) energy needs, good storage, slow delivery


2. Glycerophospholipids
    --Primary constituents of cell membranes

        -- Structure: Two fatty acids form ester linkages with the first and second hydroxyl groups of L-glycerol-3-phosphate

        -- The highly polar phosphate group may be further esterified by an alcohol; such substituent groups are called the head groups (carry charges at physiological pH) 

                -- e.g. Phosphatidylcholine
                        -- Major component of most eukaryotic cell membranes
                        -- Many prokaryotes, including E. coli, cannot synthesize this lipid; their membranes do not contain phosphatidylcholine



3. Sphingolipids
    -- A long-chain amino alcohol sphingosine
    -- A fatty acid is joined to sphingosine via an amide linkage/peptide bond rather than an ester linkage as usually seen in lipids
    -- A polar head group is connected to sphingosine by a glycosidic or phosphodiester linkage
    -- The sugar-containing glycosphingolipids (Blood Types) are found largely in the outer face of plasma membranes



4. Sterol

   -- A sterol example -- Cholesterol
          -- Hydroxyl group (polar head) in the A-ring

          -- Various nonpolar side chains         



  • Biological Membrane

1. Micelle
--Forms in the solution of amphipathic molecules that have larger head than tail


2. Liposome

    -- The central aqueous cavity can enclose dissolved molecules
    --Vesicles fuse readily with cell membranes or with each other

3. Bilayer membrane
- Hydrophilic head groups interact with water
- Hydrophobic fatty acid tails are packed inside
- Structural properties: Asymmetric 
- Physical properties :  Membrane dynamics (lateral diffusion) (transverse diffusion)

    -- Organisms adjust membrane composition

            -- Membrane fluidity is determined mainly by the fatty acid composition.

            -- More fluid membranes require shorter and more unsaturated fatty acids

            -- At higher temperatures cells need more saturated fatty acids to maintain integrity

            -- At lower temperatures cells need more unsaturated fatty acids to maintain fluidity
