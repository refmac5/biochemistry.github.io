Which of the following is true of the citric acid cycle?

  1. Oxaloacetate is used as a substrate, but is not consumed in the cycle

  2. ATP and NAD+ are allosteric regulators of the cycle

  3. Flux through the cycle is regulated by the availability of citrate synthase substrates

  4. Acetyl-CoA is a key intermediate of the cycle

  5. Flux through the cycle is decreased when oxygen is limited

Answer

A, C, and E

The cycle can be envisioned as starting with the condensation reaction of oxaloacetate and acetyl-CoA. This is a highly exergonic and key regulatory step, and flux through the entire cycle is to a large extent determined by the concentrations of the 2 substrates. The citric acid cycle is also regulated allosterically at several steps by ATP and NADH, which are inhibitors.

Acetyl-CoA is a key substrate that feeds into the citric acid cycle and a key metabolic intermediate, but it is not an intermediate of the cycle itself. The net overall reaction of the citric acid cycle is the oxidation of acetyl-CoA to 2 CO2, with no net consumption of oxaloacetate.

No step in the citric acid cycle uses O2 as a substrate; however, the entire cycle is indirectly O2-dependent, since the oxidized pyridine nucleotide NAD+ is regenerated through the electron transport chain, which utilizes O2 as the terminal electron acceptor. The cycle require 3 equivalents of NAD+ per turn, and furthermore is allosterically inhibited by high concentrations of NADH.




Match each of the following reactions of the citric acid cycle to the appropriate description:

A. α-ketoglutarate + CoA-SH → succinyl-CoA + CO2

1. inhibited by thiamine deficiency

B. succinyl-CoA → succinate + CoA-SH

2. coupled to substrate-level phosphorylation

C. citrate → isocitrate

3. substrate is prochiral

D. malate → oxaloacetate

4. coupled to NAD+ reduction

E. succinate → fumarate

5. catalyzed by an integral membrane protein

Answer

  • α-ketoglutarate + CoA-SH → succinyl-CoA + CO2 - inhibited by thiamine deficiency

  • succinyl-CoA → succinate + CoA-SH - coupled to substrate-level phosphorylation

  • citrate → isocitrate - substrate is prochiral

  • malate → oxaloacetate - coupled to NAD+ reduction

  • succinate → fumarate - catalyzed by an integral membrane protein

The following are key features of the enzymatic steps above:

  1. Aconitase catalyzes the isomerization of citrate to isocitrate. In this reaction, the prochiral citrate interacts stereospecifically with the enzyme to produce only 1 of 4 possible diastereomers of isocitrate. Thus, although citrate is a symmetrical molecule, no scrambling of isotopic labels occurs at this step, due to the stereospecificity of the reaction.

  2. The α-ketoglurate dehydrogenase complex that catalyzes the oxidative decarboxylation of α-ketoglurate to succinyl-CoA is highly analogous to the pyruvate dedydrogenase complex. Like PDH, α-ketoglurate dehydrogenase utilizes TPP and will be inhibited by thiamine deficiency.

  3. Succinyl-CoA is converted to succinate in a reaction that also forms GTP (ATP) from GDP (ADP). This is the only substrate-level phosphorylation in the citric acid cycle.

  4. Succinate dehydrogenase catalyzes the oxidation of succinate to fumarate in a reaction that uses FAD as the oxidant. The enzyme is an integral membrane protein, also known as Complex II of the electron transport chain, and the FADH2 formed in the reaction is reoxidized by ubiquinol as part of the catalytic cycle.

  5. Malate dehydrogenase catalyzes the oxidation of malate to oxaloacetate. This is 1 of 3 steps in the citric acid cycle that is coupled to NAD+ reduction, forming NADH that goes on to the electron transport chain.





Match each of the listed cofactors with its role in the reaction catalized by the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex:

A. lipoamide (oxidized lipoic acid)

1. initial electron acceptor in the oxidation of pyruvate

B. NAD+

2. oxidizes FADH2

C. thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP)

3. covalently attacks the central carbon in pyruvate

D. FAD

4. oxidizes the reduced form of lipoic acid (dihydrolipoamide)

E. coenzyme A (CoA-SH)

5. accepts an acetyl group from reduced lipoic acid

Answer

  • lipoamide (oxidized lipoic acid) - initial electron acceptor in the oxidation of pyruvate

  • NAD+ - oxidizes FADH2

  • thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP) - covalently attacks the central carbon in pyruvate

  • FAD - oxidizes the reduced form of lipoic acid (dihydrolipoamide)

  • coenzyme A (CoA-SH) - accepts an acetyl group from reduced lipoic acid
